📚 Study Hours vs Exam Score

What it shows: This scatter plot displays the relationship between hours spent studying and the resulting exam scores for 12 students.

Analysis: There is a strong positive correlation between study hours and exam scores. As study hours increase from 2 to 10 hours, exam scores consistently rise from 55 to 90. The trendline shows a slope of approximately 4.33, meaning each additional hour of study is associated with a 4.33-point increase in exam scores.

Key Insight: Students who studied 8+ hours (E, F, I, K) all scored above 83, while those studying 4 or fewer hours (A, C, H, J) scored 72 or below, highlighting the critical importance of study time.

😴 Sleep Hours vs Exam Score

What it shows: This scatter plot displays the relationship between sleep hours and exam scores, with points colored by study hours buckets (Low: 0-4, Medium: 5-7, High: 8+).

Analysis: Unlike study hours, sleep hours show no clear correlation with exam scores. Students with high scores (80-90) appear across all sleep ranges from 3-8 hours. The color coding reveals that study hours, not sleep hours, determine the score - red points (low study) cluster at low scores regardless of sleep, while blue points (high study) achieve high scores even with minimal sleep.

Key Insight: Student I (9 study hours, 3 sleep) scored 88, while student L (7 study hours, 8 sleep) scored 80 - demonstrating that study intensity outweighs sleep duration in determining exam performance.

📊 Comparative Analysis Summary

  • Most Predictive Factor: Study hours strongly predict exam scores (R² ≈ 0.85), while sleep hours show almost no predictive power.
  • Optimal Strategy: Students should prioritize 7-10 hours of study for scores above 80. Sleep between 6-8 hours is sufficient - more sleep doesn't compensate for less study.
  • Outlier Identified: Student L studied 7 hours but scored only 80, slightly below the trendline prediction, possibly indicating inefficient study methods or test anxiety.
  • Practical Recommendation: Focus on quality study time rather than sacrificing sleep - the data shows high achievers maintain good scores even with 3-6 hours of sleep when study time is high.
